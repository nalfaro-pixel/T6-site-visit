Phase 1 — Interview

Seated conversation with the department head or Title VI coordinator. Capture responses in the notes field for each question. Approximately 60 minutes.

1
Tell us about something your department has done that you’re proud of — a success with language access, community outreach, or equitable service that you think worked well and should be continued or expanded.
Opening question. Signals a collaborative tone. Listen for specific programs, initiatives, or staff efforts worth noting.
2
From your department’s scope of work, do you see languages or communities that are underserved or harder to reach? Which has been the most challenging, and what do you think makes it difficult?
Listen for specific language groups, geographic barriers, trust issues, or structural gaps. Note any communities named and what the department believes drives the gap.
3
Which tools or resources have helped you the most in meeting service needs under the Title VI Implementation Plan?
Looking backward at what is working. Note specific tools, vendors, CBO partnerships, or county resources mentioned by name.
4
What additional support or resources would help improve access and equitable service — things your department does not currently have?
Looking forward at gaps. Note whether asks are about budget, staffing, training, technology, or partnerships. This is direct resource-mapping data.
5
How do you communicate Title VI requirements to your team and ensure front-line staff are following through day to day?
Listen for whether communication is systematic (meetings, written policy, training) or informal. Note any gaps between leadership awareness and staff-level knowledge.
6
Who on your front-line staff would be a good person for us to speak with during the site walkthrough today?
Sets up Phase 3. Note the name and role. This is a natural follow-on from the team communication question.
7
Beyond the annual EOP reporting, how does your department monitor ongoing compliance with Title VI requirements?
Listen for data tracking, internal audits, staff check-ins, or complaint review processes. Absence of any monitoring is itself a finding.
8
Are there any Title VI requirements or processes that feel unclear, difficult to implement, or that your department struggles with consistently?
Asked later in the conversation once trust is established. Note specific requirements named. This is key data for the needs assessment and resource mapping.
9
Is there anything else you would want us to know, or an idea you have that would make Title VI compliance or community access more achievable across the department?
Soft close. Catches anything the structured questions did not surface and leaves the interviewee feeling heard.
